4 ** File: c_io.h

6 ** Author: K. Reek
7 **
8 ** Contributor: Jon Coles, Warren R. Carithers
9 **
10 ** Description: Declarations and descriptions of console I/O routines
11 **
12 ** These routines provide a rudimentary capability for printing to
13 ** the screen and reading from the keyboard.
14 **
15 ** Screen output:
16 ** There are two families of functions. The first provides a window
17 ** that behaves in the usual manner: writes extending beyond the right
18 ** edge of the window wrap around to the next line, the top line
19 ** scrolls off the window to make room for new lines at the bottom.
20 ** However, you may choose what part of the screen contains this
21 ** scrolling window. This allows you to print some text at fixed
22 ** locations on the screen while the rest of the screen scrolls.
23 **
24 ** The second family allows for printing at fixed locations on the
25 ** screen. No scrolling or line wrapping are done for these functions.
26 ** It is not intended that these functions be used to write in the
27 ** scrolling area of the screen.
28 **
29 ** In both sets of functions, the (x,y) coordinates are interpreted
30 ** as (column,row), with the upper left corner of the screen being
31 ** (0,0) and the lower right corner being (79,24).
32 **
33 ** The printf provided in both sets of functions has the same
34 ** conversion capabilities. Format codes are of the form:
35 **
36 ** %-0WC
37 **
38 ** where "-", "0", and "W" are all optional:
39 ** "-" is the left-adjust flag (default is right-adjust)
40 ** "0" is the zero-fill flag (default is space-fill)
41 ** "W" is a number specifying the minimum field width (default: 1 )
42 ** and "C" is the conversion type, which must be one of these:
43 ** "c" print a single character
44 ** "s" print a null-terminated string
45 ** "d" print an integer as a decimal value
46 ** "x" print an integer as a hexadecimal value
47 ** "o" print an integer as a octal value
48 **
49 ** Keyboard input:
50 ** Two functions are provided: getting a single character and getting
51 ** a newline-terminated line. A third function returns a count of
52 ** the number of characters available for immediate reading.
53 ** No conversions are provided (yet).
54 */

206 /*****************************************************************************
207 **
208 ** INPUT ROUTINES
209 **
210 ** When interrupts are enabled, a keyboard ISR collects keystrokes
211 ** and saves them until the program calls for them. If the input
212 ** queue fills, additional characters are silently discarded.
213 ** When interrupts are not enabled, keystrokes made when no input
214 ** routines have been ** called are lost. This can cause errors in
215 ** the input translation because the states of the Shift and Ctrl keys
216 ** may not be tracked accurately. If interrupts are disabled, the user
217 ** is advised to refrain from typing anything except when the program is
218 ** waiting for input.
219 */
